BioTrack is a collaborative MBON project led by Dr. Neil Hammerschlag (University of Miami / Shark Research Foundation) and supported by NOAA's [Animal Telemetry Network](https://ioos.noaa.gov/project/atn/) (ATN). It combines satellite and acoustic tracking of marine animals with environmental data and modeling to identify, monitor, and predict changes in marine biodiversity hotspots — then delivers those insights as practical products for decision-makers.

The project is currently focused on the waters of the U.S. North Atlantic. Newly acquired and historical tracking data from sharks, sea turtles, seabirds, marine mammals, and large fish are integrated into a single picture of biodiversity overlap and movement corridors. All data products will become publicly accessible through an interactive portal, supporting fisheries management, spatial planning, and marine conservation.

## Why animal tracking reveals biodiversity

Many species share the same productive ocean habitats. Wherever mobile predators converge — offshore canyon edges, upwelling zones, seamounts — so do their prey and the rest of the food web. BioTrack uses these convergence patterns to map and forecast ecologically important areas that might otherwise be invisible to fixed-station monitoring.

In its first tagging effort, BioTrack deployed satellite tags on ocean sunfish (*Mola mola*) in the North Atlantic — a species that dives across a wide depth range and aggregates near the same offshore features that support diverse communities of other species. Early data show most individuals traveling from Maine to North Carolina within short periods, and gathering near offshore canyons likely exploiting upwelling zones.

## Data access

Tracks are served through the [ATN Data Portal](https://portal.atn.ioos.us/), where managers, researchers, and the public can explore species movements and their overlap with marine protected areas and human-use zones.
