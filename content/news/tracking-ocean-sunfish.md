By tracking marine species like the ocean sunfish (*Mola mola*), **BioTrack** — a project of MBON — is uncovering key patterns in biodiversity and making marine animal-movement data accessible to stakeholders.

## Background

The Atlantic is home to a wide diversity of marine species, many of which share habitats impacted by human activities. Identifying areas important to many species — and how these hotspots intersect with human activities — is crucial for effective conservation, sustainable management, and ocean planning.

That's the mission of BioTrack: combining satellite and acoustic tracking of marine animals with environmental data and modeling to identify, monitor, and predict changes in biodiversity hotspots, then turn that into practical products for decision-makers.

## Why sunfish are ideal for tracking biodiversity

The ocean sunfish can weigh upwards of 1,000 pounds and span 10 feet fin to fin. They eat gelatinous prey, bask at the surface, yet dive into the twilight zone to forage — visiting a wide range of habitats and so revealing where biodiversity converges.

> "They are true ocean giants — mobile, visible species that use a variety of habitats. Tracking them reveals the broader hotspots where multiple species converge." — Dr. Austin Gallagher, Beneath The Waves

As the dataset grows, these movements join a larger, integrated picture that BioTrack is building for resource managers — supporting tools that forecast species presence and guide spatial planning.
